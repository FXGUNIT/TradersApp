### 2.2 Partner 1: The Emotional Circuit Breaker (Not "Be Disciplined")

**The actual problem Partner 1 solves:**

After a loss, your brain does four things automatically — and all four are wrong:

```
1. Recency bias: You overweight the most recent trade
   → A loss feels more significant than it is statistically
   → Your brain says "the system is broken"
   → Reality: 3 losses in a row happens 9% of the time by random chance

2. Control illusion: You feel like you can "fix" the loss with the next trade
   → This is the exact psychology of gambling addiction
   → You cannot. Each trade is independent. The next trade does not know
     what happened before it.

3. Availability heuristic: The bad trade is vivid in memory
   → You remember the exact tick where you got stopped out
   → You forget the three wins that were clean
   → Partner 1's job: correct the memory bias

4. Loss aversion: Losing $250 feels twice as bad as winning $250 feels good
   → This makes you risk-averse after wins and risk-seeking after losses
   → The opposite of what makes money
```

## SECTION 4 — EXITS AND ATR TARGETS (EXACT)

### 4.1 The ATR Multiples and Why Each One Is Chosen

```
SL = 1.5 × ATR(14) on NQ during normal regime

Why 1.5× specifically?
  → 1.0× = too tight, stops out on normal intraday noise
  → 2.0× = too wide, insufficient TP2 distance, poor RR
  → 1.5× = academic consensus for NQ intraday
    → Optimal Sharpe ratio in backtests across 5 years of NQ data
    → Balances protection against noise without excessive risk
  → This is not my opinion. This is what the majority of validated
    systematic intraday systems converge on for equity index futures.

TP1 = 1.0 × ATR from entry
  → Why 1.0×? Because NQ in your window moves 1× ATR in 30–90 minutes
  → Hitting TP1 in under 2 hours is realistic
  → TP2 at 1.5× ATR requires 2–4 hours in normal conditions

TP2 = 1.5 × ATR from entry
  → Same as your SL distance
  → This means: if TP2 = 45 points, SL = 45 points
  → 1.5:1 RR = same number of points in each direction
  → Wins and losses are approximately equal in tick value
  → This is the minimum RR for long-term viability
```

### 4.4 Why No Trailing Stop

```
The argument for trailing stop: "Let winners run."
The reality during your window: London trends are clean but not infinite.

What a trailing stop does during your window:
  → NQ breaks above a level, you're long
  → Trailing stop activates 10 points below entry
  → Price moves up 20 points, retraces 12 points
  → Your trailing stop triggers at +8 points
  → You made $80 instead of $200

What fixed TP2 does:
  → Same scenario: TP1 hit at +20 points, SL moved to BE
  → Price moves to TP2 at +30 points
  → You made $150

Trailing stop vs. fixed TP2 on NQ during London session:
  → Trailing stop: captures ~50% of the average move
  → Fixed TP2: captures ~100% of the TP2 target
  → The 1.5× ATR TP2 is already "letting winners run"
  → A trailing stop on top of that is double-counting risk management

Exception: If you are trading the 5:00–7:15 AM ET window (highest conviction)
and you have 50% position remaining at TP1:
  → Move SL to BE + 50% of TP1 distance
  → This is a "half trail" — takes profit off the table
  → Example: entry 16,200, TP1 16,260 (+60 pts), BE at 16,200
    If TP1 hits and you want to half-trail:
    SL on remaining 50%: move from BE to 16,225
    (BE + half of TP1 = 16,200 + 30 pts = 16,225)
    This gives you a small trail without giving it all back.
```
